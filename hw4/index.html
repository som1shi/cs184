Overview

In this project we built a real-time cloth simulator from scratch. We modeled cloth as a grid of point masses connected by structural, shearing, and bending springs, then integrated the system forward in time using Verlet integration. We added collision handling for spheres and planes, self-collision via spatial hashing, and a suite of GLSL shaders — Blinn-Phong, texture mapping, bump and displacement mapping, and a mirror shader — to render the cloth with physically plausible materials.

The most interesting insight was how much visual richness comes from the shader pipeline rather than the physics. The same cloth mesh with different shaders looks completely different — from a rough woven fabric to a perfectly reflective mirror — while the underlying spring-mass simulation stays the same.

Part 1: Masses and Springs

We model the cloth as a grid of point masses connected by three types of springs. Structural springs connect each mass to its immediate horizontal and vertical neighbors, resisting stretching and compression along the grid axes. Shearing springs connect diagonal neighbors to resist in-plane shear deformation. Bending springs connect every other neighbor horizontally and vertically to provide out-of-plane bending resistance, giving the cloth a stiffness that prevents it from folding too sharply.

Part 2: Simulation via Numerical Integration

We use Verlet integration to step the simulation forward in time. At each timestep we compute the total force on each point mass — gravity, spring forces, and any external forces — then update positions as x(t+dt) = x(t) + (1 - d) * (x(t) - x(t-dt)) + a * dt², where d is the damping fraction. After integration we apply a position correction constraint that prevents any spring from stretching more than 10% beyond its rest length.

The baseline parameters used throughout are ks = 5000 N/m, density = 15 g/cm², and damping = 0.20%.

Spring Constant (ks)

The spring constant controls how stiffly the springs resist stretching. With a low ks (100 N/m), the cloth has visible ripples as it swings down — most noticeable in the first second as it drops — and sags more deeply between the two pinned corners at rest since the springs offer little resistance to gravity. It also takes a longer time to settle. With a high ks (50000 N/m), the cloth swings down as a nearly undisturbed sheet and hangs in a shallower, tighter arc at rest.

Density

Density affects each point mass's weight via F = ma. Higher density means more mass and therefore more gravitational force relative to the spring restoration forces. At the default density the cloth swings with a natural, fabric-like motion. At density = 500 g/cm², gravity dominates over the springs — the cloth is yanked downward hard, the springs near the pinned corners are visibly stretched, and the cloth ripples more violently during the initial drop. At rest, the higher density cloth also hangs lower with a deeper drape.

Damping

Damping controls how quickly kinetic energy dissipates from the system. The screenshot below shows 0% damping captured 5 seconds into the simulation — at the same point where the default cloth has fully settled. With no energy dissipation the mechanical energy from the initial fall is never lost, so the cloth continues to oscillate indefinitely. Conversely, a high damping value (1%) causes the cloth to fall slowly and gently with no oscillations.

Part 3: Handling Collisions with Other Objects

Implementation

For sphere collisions, we check if a point mass is inside the sphere by computing the distance from the point mass to the sphere's origin. If that distance is less than or equal to the radius, the point mass has intersected the sphere. We extend the direction from the origin through the point mass out to the sphere's surface to find the tangent point, compute the correction vector from the point mass's last position to that tangent point, and apply it scaled by (1 - friction).

For plane collisions, we check if a point mass crossed the plane between this time step and the last by looking at which side of the plane the current and previous positions are on (using the dot product with the plane normal). If the signs differ, the point mass crossed over. We find the tangent point where the path intersects the plane, offset it slightly above the surface using SURFACE_OFFSET on the original side, and apply the correction vector from last position to this offset point, again scaled by (1 - friction).

Sphere Collisions at Different ks

With a low ks of 500, the cloth is very loose and drapes closely around the sphere since the springs don't resist stretching much. At the default ks = 5000, the cloth drapes more naturally with some stiffness. At ks = 50000, the cloth is much stiffer and doesn't conform as tightly to the sphere — it almost looks like a stiff sheet resting on top rather than draping around it.

Part 4: Handling Self-Collisions

Implementation

We implemented self-collisions using spatial hashing to avoid the O(n²) cost of checking every pair of point masses. The idea is to partition 3D space into a grid of boxes and only check for collisions between point masses that share the same box.

In hash_position, we compute the box dimensions as w = 3 * width / num_width_points, h = 3 * height / num_height_points, and t = max(w, h). We truncate each coordinate to its box index using floor and combine them into a single unique float. In build_spatial_map, we loop through all point masses, hash their positions, and group them into buckets in an unordered map. In self_collide, for a given point mass we look up all other point masses in the same bucket. For any candidate within 2 * thickness distance, we compute a correction vector that pushes them apart to exactly 2 * thickness. We average all corrections and scale by 1 / simulation_steps to keep things stable.

Cloth Falling and Folding

Varying Density

With low density, the cloth is lighter so it falls more gently and produces fewer, wider folds. With high density, the cloth is heavier and falls faster, creating more tightly packed folds and creases as it piles on itself.

Varying ks

With low ks, the springs are weak so the cloth crumples easily and folds into itself with lots of small wrinkles. With high ks, the cloth is much stiffer and resists folding — it tends to make broader, smoother folds and holds its shape more as it comes to rest.

Part 5: Shaders

Overview

A shader program is a small, isolated program that runs in parallel on the GPU, processing sections of the graphics pipeline. Instead of doing all lighting calculations on the CPU, shaders let us offload that work to the GPU, which can run thousands of these small programs simultaneously.

There are two main types that work together. Vertex shaders run first and operate on each vertex of the mesh — they transform vertex positions and normals from model space to world/screen space and pass along interpolated values like UV coordinates and normals. Fragment shaders then run on each pixel-sized fragment after rasterization, taking in the interpolated per-vertex data from the vertex shader and computing the final color of that pixel. By combining these two stages we can create a wide variety of lighting and material effects in real time.

Blinn-Phong Shading Model

The Blinn-Phong model breaks lighting into three components. The ambient term is a constant base illumination so nothing is ever completely black — it approximates indirect light bouncing around the scene. The diffuse term models light scattering equally in all directions off a rough surface, depending on the angle between the surface normal and the light direction (n · l). The specular term creates shiny highlights using the half-vector h between the light and view directions — the sharper the falloff (controlled by exponent p), the shinier the material looks.

Full equation: L = ka·Ia + kd·(I/r²)·max(0, n·l) + ks·(I/r²)·max(0, n·h)p

We used ka = 0.1, kd = 1.0, ks = 0.5, Ia = (1,1,1), and p = 100.

Texture Mapping

For texture mapping, we sample from a texture using the UV coordinates interpolated across the surface from the vertex shader. We used a custom texture loaded into u_texture_1.

Bump Mapping and Displacement Mapping

Both techniques use a height map texture to add surface detail, but they work differently.

Bump mapping only modifies the normal vectors used in the lighting calculation, creating the illusion of surface detail without actually changing the geometry. We compute how the height changes in the u and v directions, construct a local-space normal from those derivatives, and transform it back to model space using the TBN (tangent-bitangent-normal) matrix.

Displacement mapping actually moves the vertex positions along their normals based on the height map, in addition to modifying the normals like bump mapping does. This means the silhouette and geometry of the object genuinely changes.

We used texture_3.png for the renders below.

Comparing the two: bump mapping gives a convincing illusion of detail from most viewing angles, but the silhouette stays perfectly smooth — you can tell the geometry hasn't actually changed. Displacement mapping physically deforms the mesh, so you can see the bumps in the object's outline. The tradeoff is that displacement mapping requires enough mesh resolution to represent the detail.

Mesh Coarseness Comparison

With bump mapping, the coarseness of the sphere mesh doesn't really matter — since we're only modifying normals in the fragment shader, both the coarse and fine meshes look similar. But with displacement mapping, the coarse mesh (-o 16 -a 16) doesn't have enough vertices to capture the height map detail, so the result looks blocky and rough. The fine mesh (-o 128 -a 128) has plenty of vertices and the displacement looks much smoother and more detailed.

Mirror Shader

For the mirror shader, we compute the outgoing eye-ray direction from the camera to the fragment, reflect it across the surface normal, and then sample the environment cubemap in that reflected direction. This gives us a simple but effective mirror material.

Extra Credit

Wind

We added a spatially varying wind force to the simulation. In simulate(), after gravity is applied but before spring forces are computed, we add a wind force to each point mass based on its position and a static time variable that increments by delta_t each step.

The gust magnitude is computed as: gust = 1.0 + 0.5 * sin(0.5 * x + 1.0 * t) + 0.3 * cos(0.5 * y + 0.8 * t)

The constant 1.0 baseline ensures the wind always blows in the same direction (rather than oscillating back and forth), while the sin and cos terms add gusts that vary across the cloth's surface and over time. This means different regions of the cloth feel different amounts of wind at the same instant, which produces the billowing characteristic of real wind. The final force is Vector3D(0.5, 0, 1.0) * wind_strength * gust, blowing diagonally in the xz-plane.

Without wind, the cloth settles into a static drape. With wind, the cloth is pushed sideways and ripples continuously, never fully coming to rest.

Cloth Tearing

We added the ability for the cloth to tear apart when its springs are stretched too far. In simulate(), at the end of each timestep, we remove any spring whose current length exceeds 2.5× its rest length. Once a spring is gone, the two point masses it connected are no longer held together. If enough springs break in the same region, the cloth physically separates into pieces.

To showcase this we ran scene/sphere.json with the Provot constraint temporarily disabled, letting springs stretch freely under contact forces until they reached the breaking threshold. The cloth first lands on the sphere and drapes over it intact, with visible tension building in the springs wrapping around the curvature. The highest-stretch points, where the cloth curves most sharply over the sphere, are the first to reach the threshold. Once those springs break, holes open up in the cloth, and the torn pieces fall away from the sphere. The result is a simple, realistic tearing effect.
